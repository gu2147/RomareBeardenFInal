ABOUT THE ARTIST

"Art is always made from other art."
~Romare Bearden




In the early stages of his career, Bearden worked as a political cartoonist, creating works for African American publications that addressed issues of social injustice and inequality. These early forays set the stage for his lifelong engagement with political and social themes, which would continue to shape his art.

In the mid 1930s Bearden studied art history and philosophy at the Sorbonne in Paris, where he was exposed to European art movements and influential artists like Pablo Picasso and Henri Matisse. His return to the U.S. in the 1950s marked a period of exploration, wherein he experimented with abstract expressionism and modernist aesthetics. Yet, he soon returned to more representational imagery, finding that the vibrant and deeply rooted stories of the African American community could best be expressed through figurative art.

Bearden's distinctive collage technique emerged in the 1960s, a period marked by the civil rights movement and immense social upheaval. Using magazine clippings, fabric, and paint, he assembled intricate compositions that fused surrealism, cubism, and African art. His works often depicted scenes from urban life, rural southern landscapes, and historical or mythological themes. One of his best-known series, "The Odyssey," was inspired by Homer's epic poem and interpreted the Greek myth through the lens of the African diaspora, emphasizing the universality of the human journey.

In addition to his visual artistry, Bearden was an influential intellectual and advocate for African American art. He co-founded the Spiral Group, a collective of Black artists committed to exploring how art could contribute to social change during the civil rights movement. His involvement in organizations like the Studio Museum in Harlem and the Black Academy of Arts and Letters helped to shape the development of Black art and culture in America. Romare Bearden passed away on March 12, 1988, but his legacy endures. His work continues to captivate and inspire, as it embodies the rich tapestry of African American culture and the universal quest for identity and belonging. He remains celebrated for his ability to merge diverse influences into art that speaks to the shared human condition. Bearden's collages, paintings, and prints are held in high regard by museums and collectors worldwide, ementing his position as one of the foremost American artists of the 20th century.
